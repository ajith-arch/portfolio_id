Re‑Architecting Product Taxonomy
to Align with Student Search Behavior

How backend systems redesign aligned product discovery with student intent — increasing cart saves from 24% to 32% and significantly improving revenue.

Role Senior UI/UX Designer
Organization University of Utah Campus Store
Platform NetSuite E‑Commerce Platform
Focus Product Taxonomy, Search Behavior, Conversion
Primary Metric Cart saves: 24% → 32%
Scope Apparel, Tech, Accessories, Drinkware, Essentials

Problem

Students were spending 3–4 minutes just to identify the right product, despite a functional UI. Conversion was limited by how products were categorized, not how the interface looked.

Goal

Align backend product taxonomy with real student search behavior so that “short sleeve”, “crew neck”, or “polo” lead directly to relevant results — without redesigning the storefront UI.

Solution

Re‑architected NetSuite categories around intent‑driven subcategories, added AI‑assisted tagging during PDP creation, and standardized taxonomy rules across apparel, tech, and accessories.

Impact

Cart saves increased from 24% to 32% (33% relative lift), product identification time dropped below 1 minute, and we improved revenue without changing a single visible UI component.

Context

A University‑Scale Storefront with Underperforming Conversion

The University of Utah Campus Store operates a full‑scale e‑commerce platform serving students, faculty, alumni, and visitors across apparel, laptops, accessories, drinkware, and academic essentials. Despite steady traffic and strong brand demand, conversion metrics were underperforming — even though heuristics suggested the UI was “fine”.

Navigation looked familiar. Filters existed. Search was available. But users still took too long to find what they needed, and many never made it to checkout. The friction wasn’t obvious in pixels — it was buried in how the system organized products.

The Hidden Problem

When Students Think in Product Types, but the System Doesn’t

Deeper investigation revealed a mismatch between how students searched and how the backend categorized products. Students thought in everyday product terms; the system thought in administrative attributes.

How Students Searched

  • “short sleeve”
  • “crew neck”
  • “polo”
  • “basic tee”
  • “athletic fit”

How NetSuite Was Structured

  • Gender
  • Brand
  • Main category
  • Color
  • Size

Intent‑based product types were not modeled as structured subcategories in NetSuite. As a result:

  • Search results were incomplete and inconsistent.
  • Filters didn’t align with how students naturally described products.
  • Product identification time averaged 3–4 minutes.

The issue wasn’t interface design — it was category architecture.

Research

How Students Actually Shop

We conducted moderated usability testing with 50 students, asking them to find and order specific products, narrate how they searched, and explain their navigation decisions.

Tasks

  • Find and purchase a specific apparel item.
  • Locate a laptop with certain specs.
  • Identify a gift item under a price constraint.

Key Findings

  • 80% began with product‑type keywords, not gender or brand.
  • Very few navigated directly through Men/Women category trees.
  • Students expected “short sleeve” or “crew neck” to act as primary filters.
  • Identification time averaged 3–4 minutes per item.
  • Friction led to delayed cart actions and abandoned sessions.

Search logs confirmed this behavior: repeated product‑type queries returned inconsistent sets of products, validating a taxonomy misalignment rather than a UI flaw.

Strategic Intervention

Re‑Architecting NetSuite Around Intent‑Driven Subcategories

Instead of relying solely on gender and brand hierarchies, we redesigned the product taxonomy inside NetSuite to standardize intent‑driven subcategories across all major product types.

We introduced a consistent subcategory framework for:

  • Apparel
  • Footwear
  • Headwear
  • Drinkware
  • Laptops
  • Accessories

For apparel, subcategories included:

  • Short Sleeve
  • Long Sleeve
  • Polo
  • Crew Neck
  • Activewear
  • Basic

PDP creators are now required to assign both a main category and an intent‑driven subcategory during product creation. This ensured consistent mapping across search, filters, and merchandising views.

AI‑Assisted Categorization

Using AI to Recommend the Right Category, Not Replace Judgment

To reduce manual effort and increase accuracy, we implemented AI‑assisted category suggestions directly in the PDP workflow.

When media is uploaded:

  • AI analyzes the product image and metadata.
  • Suggests an optimal subcategory (e.g., “Short Sleeve”, “Crew Neck”).
  • Allows merchandisers to override the suggestion.
  • Auto‑populates tagging fields to keep taxonomy consistent.

Publishing became faster, classification more consistent, and taxonomy rules easier to follow without requiring deep system knowledge.

Validation

Proving the Impact Without Changing the UI

After deploying the new taxonomy, we ran a second round of usability testing and a live A/B test, this time focusing on behavior and performance with no frontend UI changes.

  • Product identification time reduced from 3–4 minutes to under 1 minute.
  • Direct add‑to‑cart actions increased as students reached relevant products faster.
  • Navigation felt intuitive — filters finally matched how students described what they wanted.

We then ran a live A/B test:

  • Variant A — Existing category structure.
  • Variant B — Intent‑driven subcategory architecture.

No visual UI changes were made — only the backend taxonomy shifted.

What This Really Means

Systems‑Level UX, Not Just Screen‑Level Design

This project sits at the intersection of UX, data, and systems architecture. It demonstrates how backend decisions quietly shape every “simple” shopping experience.

  • Systems‑level product thinking instead of screen‑by‑screen tweaks.
  • Backend architecture as a primary UX surface.
  • Cross‑team collaboration between merchandising, engineering, and analytics.
  • AI integrated into workflow as assistive intelligence, not automation theatre.
  • Data‑driven decision‑making tied directly to revenue and retention.

This was not a UI enhancement. It was commerce infrastructure optimization.

Reflection

Designing Where Users Never See

This experience reinforced a critical lesson: user friction is not always visible in the interface. Sometimes the most impactful design decisions happen inside systems architecture.

By aligning product taxonomy with student search behavior, we transformed product discovery, improved operational efficiency, and drove measurable revenue impact — without redesigning a single interface component.

Future enhancements could include a dedicated systems architecture diagram, taxonomy mapping visualizations, micro‑interactions for AI suggestions, and a revenue impact infographic — deepening the narrative for stakeholders who care about infrastructure as much as UI.
