Instructional design case study

Designing a Training System to Improve NetSuite Product Publishing Efficiency

Context

University of Utah Campus Store · 50+ operational employees · Oracle NetSuite ERP

Role

Instructional Designer (with UX-driven approach)

Project type

Workflow Training · eLearning · Performance Support

I designed a performance-focused learning system to help employees efficiently publish products in NetSuite. The solution focused on reducing complexity, improving task clarity, and enabling independent task completion.

The training system reduced product publishing time from 30–40 minutes to under 10 minutes, while improving consistency and reducing dependency on experienced staff.

Problem

Workflow friction and instructional gap

Publishing products in NetSuite required navigating multiple disconnected tabs, manually managing images, and remembering non-intuitive steps.

This resulted in:

  • High cognitive load
  • Frequent errors and rework
  • Long task completion time (30–40 minutes)
  • Heavy reliance on experienced employees

Instructional gap

There was no structured training system. Employees relied on verbal instructions, trial-and-error, and shadowing experienced staff.

Employees needed guidance at the moment of action, not just upfront training. The solution focused on embedding learning directly into the workflow rather than separating learning from doing.

This was not only a usability issue—it was a performance and learning problem.

Learning goal

Primary outcome

Enable employees to independently publish a complete product (including images, content, and pricing) in under 10 minutes without assistance.

Learning objectives

After completing the training

Employees will be able to:

  • Identify all required fields for product publishing
  • Navigate the NetSuite workflow efficiently without backtracking
  • Upload and manage product images correctly
  • Complete product publishing without errors
  • Validate product details before publishing
Research and insights

What we observed

I conducted informal observations and worked closely with internal teams to understand workflow challenges.

Key insights

  • Employees relied on memory instead of guidance
  • Most errors occurred during image handling and content entry
  • Users frequently switched between tabs to verify steps
  • New employees took significantly longer to onboard
Instructional design framework

Instructional Design Framework

This project followed a structured instructional design approach to ensure the training solution addressed both learning and performance outcomes:

Analysis

Identified workflow inefficiencies, performance gaps, and lack of structured training through observation and collaboration with internal teams.

Design

Defined clear learning goals and objectives, and structured content around real task workflows.

Development

Created microlearning modules, interactive simulations, job aids, and video tutorials to support different learning needs.

Implementation

Integrated training into day-to-day workflows, enabling employees to learn while performing tasks.

Evaluation

Measured improvements in task completion time, error reduction, and overall task success.

Training design approach

Performance-first instruction

I approached this as a performance-first instructional design problem, not just documentation.

Strategy

  • Reduce cognitive load through structured learning
  • Break tasks into smaller, learnable steps
  • Provide real-time support during execution
  • Combine learning and doing
Solution

A multi-layered training system

Instead of designing a single long training module, the learning experience was broken into microlearning units to match how employees perform tasks in short, repeated workflows. This approach improved retention, reduced overwhelm, and allowed learners to access only what they needed at the right moment.

Microlearning modules

Short, focused modules (2–3 minutes each): Product Basics; Image Upload and Management; Content and Descriptions; Pricing and Validation. Designed for quick learning and easy recall.

Interactive simulation

A guided, step-by-step simulation of the publishing process: users learn by doing, with click-through interactions and immediate feedback. This helps reduce fear of making mistakes.

Job aid (performance support)

A one-page checklist employees can use during work: step-by-step workflow, key reminders, and error-prevention tips. Designed for real-time usage.

Video tutorials

Short screen recordings covering common mistakes, best practices, and edge cases.

UX and instructional design

Integration

This project combines instructional design with UX principles:

  • Reduced cognitive load through structured steps
  • Chunked information into manageable units
  • Used visual cues to guide attention
  • Designed for recognition over recall

Result: learning feels intuitive, not overwhelming.

Impact

Outcomes

Before training

  • 30–40 minutes per product
  • High error rate
  • Dependency on experienced staff

After training

  • ~10 minutes per product
  • Improved first-attempt success
  • Faster onboarding
  • Increased process consistency
  • Improved first-attempt task success rate
  • Reduced reliance on peer support
  • Increased learner confidence during task execution
  • Faster onboarding for new employees
Key takeaways

What this project reinforced

  • Instructional design can directly improve operational efficiency
  • Training is most effective when integrated with real workflows
  • Combining UX thinking with learning design creates better outcomes

This project demonstrates how structured learning experiences can transform complex systems into efficient, scalable workflows.
